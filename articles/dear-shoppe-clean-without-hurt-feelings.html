Dear Shoppe: I want to be clean, but I don’t want to hurt anyone’s feelings

Hygiene conversations go sideways when they turn into character judgments. Here’s how to talk about comfort and care without shame.

  • Aftercare
  • Respect
  • Communication
  • Comfort

Dear Shoppe,
I’m close to someone I care about. Sometimes there’s a smell or taste that throws me off. I don’t want to shame them or make them feel gross — but I also don’t want to ignore my own body’s reactions. What do I do?

First: remove the accusation

Smell and taste aren’t morality. They’re information: sweat, timing, diet, stress, soap residue, fabric, hormones, or a system that’s currently irritated.

Lead with “us,” not “you”

The cleanest approach is collaborative: “Can we do a quick reset together?” or “Can we do a rinse / wipe / water break before we keep going?” You’re not naming a flaw. You’re building a ritual.

Try: “I want this to feel good for both of us. Can we do a quick comfort reset?”

Make it specific and physical

  • “Can we grab water?”
  • “Want a warm washcloth?”
  • “Want to freshen up together?”
  • “Can we slow down for a second?”

If something seems truly off

If the smell is new, strong, fishy, painful, or paired with itching/burning, treat it as a health signal — not a hygiene issue. Encourage evaluation without drama.

Not medical advice. Avoid shame. If symptoms persist, get evaluated. Comfort and respect matter more than “freshness.”
